A low overall model p-value and a moderate/low Adjusted measure different things, so they can absolutely occur together.

What the Overall F-test p-value Means

Your model’s p-value:

comes from the overall F-test:

  • Null hypothesis

  • Alternative:
    At least one predictor has a nonzero relationship with win_pct.

Because the p-value is very small, you reject the null hypothesis and conclude:

The predictors collectively improve prediction beyond a model with no predictors.

That only tells you the model has real explanatory power, not that it explains all or even most of the variance.

What Adjusted Means

Your adjusted :

means:

After accounting for the number of predictors, the model explains about 48% of the variability in winning percentage.

That is actually fairly reasonable for real-world sports data.

An does not need to be close to 1 for a model to be statistically significant.

Why Both Can Happen Together

These two ideas coexist because:

  • Statistical significance asks:

“Is there evidence of a relationship?”

“How much variation do we explain?”

A model can explain only a moderate amount of variation but still do so very consistently.

Intuition

Imagine this:

  • Winning percentage depends on many things not in your model:

    • defense,

    • injuries,

    • coaching,

    • player availability,

    • schedule strength,

    • clutch performance,

    • chemistry,

    • etc.

So even a good basketball model may never get near:

But if rebounds and turnovers consistently affect winning, the regression detects that relationship clearly, giving a low p-value.

Important Point

A statistically significant model does not imply:

  • excellent prediction,

  • high practical importance,

  • or near-perfect fit.

It only means:

The relationship is unlikely to be due to random chance.

In your case:

  • The model is statistically meaningful,

  • but still leaves about half the variability unexplained,

  • which is normal in sports analytics.
